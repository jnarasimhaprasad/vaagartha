Ādi Parva · Episode 1 · Scene 1

Naimiṣāraṇya

The Storyteller

The Storyteller

Evening had descended softly upon the house.

The day, with its many small demands, had at last loosened its hold.

Appaa’s office work was done.

Neelakrishna had finished his lessons.

Niveditaa had only moments ago stepped away from her easel, after adding a few final touches to a half-finished painting. A faint streak of blue still lingered on her fingers.

Appaa spread a mat on the floor and sat down with a long, unhurried stretch, easing the day out of his shoulders and back. For a few quiet minutes, he glanced through something on his phone, scrolling without urgency, the pale light briefly touching his face.

Then the screen went dark.

He placed the phone aside.

Silence slowly filled the room.

Not the silence of emptiness,

but of rest.

The kind that comes when duties are done, voices grow gentle, and no one is in a hurry to be anywhere else.

Neelakrishna came first and sat beside Appaa.

A moment later, Niveditaa joined them and settled on the other side.

For a while, no one spoke.

Appaa noticed them.

A quiet smile touched his face.

“Hm?” he asked softly.

Neelakrishna hesitated only for a moment.

“Story?”

Appaa’s smile widened.

“Mahābhārata?”

Both nodded.

Niveditaa leaned a little closer.

“We like it when you tell it, Appaa.”

Appaa raised an eyebrow.

“More than books? More than videos?”

Niveditaa smiled.

“Books don't wait for us.
Videos don't either.
But with you...
the story waits for us.”

Neelakrishna added, in his practical way,

“With you, we can stop you.”

Niveditaa laughed.

“Yes.
We can wander.
Ask questions.
Come back again.
The story never minds.
And somehow...
it feels alive.”

Appaa looked at both of them for a long, quiet moment.

A gentle smile spread across his face.

“Do you know...”

He paused.

“We've just done something our ancestors did too.”

Neelakrishna looked puzzled.

“What do you mean, Appaa?”

Appaa glanced around the room.

“Just a little while ago...
each of us was doing something different.
You were studying.”

He looked at Niveditaa.

“You were painting.
And I was finishing my work.”

His eyes returned to both of them.

“But now...”

He smiled.

“Look at us.”

Neelakrishna looked around.

“We're all sitting together.”

Niveditaa added softly,

“And we're waiting for a story.”

Appaa nodded.

“Yes.”

He paused.

“That is how these stories once lived.
People gathered like this.
Someone narrated.
Others listened...
asked...
wondered...
interrupted.”

For a few moments, no one spoke.

Then Appaa continued.

“Long ago, in the sacred forest of Naimiṣāraṇya, many ṛṣis were gathered in the āśrama of the great kulapati Shaunaka.
They were in the midst of a long satra—
a great sacrificial session that stretched across years.”

Niveditaa listened quietly.

Appaa continued.

“One day, during a time of rest, when the sages were seated together in calm conversation, a guest arrived.”

“Who?” asked Neelakrishna.

“A storyteller.
Not an ordinary storyteller.
A paurāṇika.
One who carried stories and histories across lands.
His name was Ugraśrava Sauti.
He had travelled far—
through forests,
across sacred tīrthas,
from one holy place to another.”

Appaa paused.

“When Sauti finally reached Shaunaka's āśrama...”

His voice grew quieter.

“The sages were happy to see him.
They knew him as a paurāṇika who wandered across Bhārata, carrying sacred stories.
No one hurried to ask him anything.
They welcomed him.
They offered him water.
They invited him to sit.
He had travelled a long way.
So they simply let him rest.”

Silence filled the room for a few moments.

“It was only after the dust of the road had settled...
and the weariness had gently left him...
that one of the ṛṣis smiled and asked—
‘From where have you come, O Sauti?
Where all have you travelled?’”

Appaa paused again.

Then he continued.

“Sauti bowed to the sages and replied—
‘I have come from the great Sarpa-satra of King Janamejaya, son of Parīkṣit.’”

Neelakrishna straightened.

“The snake sacrifice?”

Appaa nodded.

“Yes.”

He paused.

“Sauti had gone to the great Sarpa-satra of King Janamejaya.
For many days, the sacrifice continued.
And there...
he sat among the listeners...
while Maharṣi Vaiśampāyana, the foremost disciple of Bhagavān Vyāsa...
narrated the Mahābhārata exactly as he had received it from the great sage Vyāsa.”

For a few moments, Appaa said nothing.

Niveditaa spoke softly.

“So...
He first received the story...
before he began carrying it.”

Appaa smiled.

“Yes.”

For a few moments, no one spoke.

Then Appaa continued.

“But Sauti did not come straight here from Janamejaya's yajña.
He travelled for some time.
He visited many tīrthas and sacred places.”

Neelakrishna was listening intently now.

“Among those places,” Appaa said, “he went to a sacred region called Samantapañcaka.”

Neelakrishna asked quietly,

“What is special about that place?”

Appaa looked at him.

A quiet smile touched his face.

“That...”

He paused.

“…is the very land of Kurukṣetra.”

The room fell silent.

For a while, neither of the children spoke.

Then Niveditaa said softly,

“So...
He heard the story...
And then he went there...
To the very land...
Where Kṛṣṇa spoke the Gītā to Arjuna...
Where the great war of Kurukṣetra was fought...
Where countless warriors gave their lives...”

Appaa said nothing.

A faint smile touched his face.
