Ādi Parva · Episode 2 · Scene 1

Saramā

Saramā's Warning

Saramā's Warning

The next evening…

The children quietly settled around Appaa once more.

Neelakrishna was already looking beyond yesterday’s story.

But part of Niveditaa still lingered among yesterday’s trees.

Neelakrishna smiled.

“Akka… are you still thinking about the two trees?”

She nodded.

“I don’t know why… but I keep seeing them.”

Appaa smiled.

“Good.
Then today…
let me show you a forest.”

Neelakrishna said,

“You’ve already shown us one, Appaa… Naimiṣāraṇya.”

“Not that forest.
Another one.
A forest
woven of stories…
and stories within stories.”

Neelakrishna’s eyes lit up.

“You mean… the Mahābhārata has many more stories within it?
I like that!”

Niveditaa looked thoughtful.

“But Appaa…
won’t we lose our way?”

Appaa smiled.

“Not if we keep walking with Sage Vyāsa.”

Neelakrishna leaned forward.

“Now I’m curious, Appaa…
Does the Mahābhārata begin with the Pāṇḍavas and Kauravas…
or with one of those other stories?”

Appaa smiled.

“With one of those.”

Neelakrishna blinked.

“So…
before the main story begins…
Vyāsa tells us another story?”

“Yes.”

He paused.

“Our elders gave such stories
a beautiful name.
Upākhyāna.”

Neelakrishna smiled.

“Then tell me, Appaa…
The very first story…
I suppose it must be about some great person.
Isn’t it?”

“Yes.
Very important indeed.
A mother.
Her name was Saramā.”

“Who was she?
A queen?
A princess?”

Appaa said

“Saramā was a dog…
the hound of heaven…
who somehow found her way to earth
along with her puppy.”

Both the children sat quietly.

Appaa continued.

“We are now in the time of King Janamejaya…
the great-grandson of Arjuna.
At Kurukṣetra,
he was performing a great yajña
along with his brothers—
Śrutasena, Ugrasena, and Bhīmasena.
Many ṛṣis sat around the sacred fires.
The chanting of the Vedas
rose and fell
through the yajñaśālā.
Just then…
a little puppy wandered into the yajñaśālā.”

Neelakrishna smiled.

“A puppy?”

Appaa nodded.

“It wandered here and there…
sniffing around with innocent curiosity.
But suddenly…
Janamejaya’s brothers drove it away and struck it.
With a frightened yelp,
It ran straight to its mother.
His mother was Saramā.
She looked at her little one.
‘Why are you crying?’
she asked.
‘Who struck you?’
‘Janamejaya’s brothers…’
the little puppy whimpered.
‘They struck me.’
Saramā listened quietly.
Then she asked,
‘You must have done something.
What was it?’
The little puppy looked up at her.
‘I did nothing.
I did not even look at the sacred offerings.
I did not go near them.’
Saramā remained silent for a moment.
Her little one’s pain weighed upon her heart.
Then she turned…
and walked towards the king’s yajñaśālā.”

“A dog…
walked into a king’s sacrifice?” whispered Neelakrishna.

“She did.
She stood before Janamejaya and asked,
‘My son did no wrong.
Why was he beaten?’”

Appaa paused.

“No one answered.
Then Saramā said,
‘Since you have hurt one who caused no harm to you…
an unseen fear shall come upon you.’
Having thus spoken,
Saramā turned.
She asked for nothing more.
She simply walked away.”

The room was quiet.

“But Appaa…
why didn’t Janamejaya answer her?”

“Janamejaya was under the vows of the yajña.
During the sacrifice,
he had to remain silent.”

After a while, Appaa continued.

Saramā had gone.

But her words had not.

They remained with Janamejaya.

The king returned to Hastināpura.

Yet the mother’s words lingered in his heart.

He began searching for a worthy purohita—

someone who could help him if that unseen danger should one day arrive.

Time passed.

One day, while out on a royal hunt, Janamejaya came upon the āśrama of a sage named Śrutaśrava.

The sage lived a life of tapas.

Beside him was his son,

Somaśrava—

a young ascetic, devoted to study and discipline.

Janamejaya bowed to the sage.

“Bhagavan,”

he said,

“May your son become my royal priest.”

Śrutaśrava looked kindly at the king.

“My son is learned.
He is steadfast in tapas.
Such is the power of his tapas
that if ever there is a misfortune
he cannot help you overcome,
it can only be one
that comes from Lord Mahādeva Himself.”

He paused.

“But remember one thing.
If ever a Brāhmaṇa asks him for anything,
he will never refuse.
If you can accept that,
take him with you.”

Janamejaya bowed once again.

“So be it, Bhagavan.”

He gladly accepted Somaśrava as his purohita.

Returning to Hastināpura, he introduced Somaśrava to his brothers.

“From this day,
he shall guide us.
Whatever he asks of us,
let us do without hesitation.”

His brothers agreed.

Then, once again,

the duties of a king called him.

Janamejaya marched to Takṣaśilā,

brought that land under his rule,

and returned in victory.

The seasons moved on.

Life seemed to flow as before.

Only somewhere,

quietly,

Saramā’s words waited.

Neither of the children spoke for a while.

“Appaa…
I thought kings listened only to other kings…
or to very important people.”

“Not Janamejaya.
He took the dog’s words seriously.”

Appaa nodded.

“He did.”

Silence lingered in the room.

Niveditaa spoke softly.

“Appaa…
yesterday…
we spoke about the two trees.”

Appaa nodded.

“Yes.”

“I think…
Saramā stood beneath the tree of dharma.”

Appaa looked at her,

but said nothing.

Neelakrishna thought for a moment.

“Because…
she was angry.
But she didn’t let her anger decide first.”

“She first wanted to know
what had really happened,”

said Niveditaa.

Appaa smiled.

“Go on.”

Neelakrishna continued.

“And Janamejaya…
he could have become angry too.
A king being questioned by a dog…”

Niveditaa nodded.

“But he didn’t.”

“No,” said Appaa quietly.

“Neither of them
allowed manyu
to choose for them.”

After a while, Neelakrishna said quietly,

“So…
this is the very first upākhyāna.”

Appaa looked at the two children.

“Yes.
We have entered
the forest of the Mahābhārata.”
