Upamanyu

Upamanyu — The Well of Vision

Upamanyu — The Well of Vision

The next evening…

The children gathered beside Appaa once again.

Neelakrishna looked at him expectantly.

“Appaa…
Yesterday you said Ṛṣi Dhaumya had three students.
We met Āruṇi.
Who is the second?”

“His name was Upamanyu,”

said Appaa.

“His story begins…
with a herd of cows.”

“A herd of cows?”

asked Neelakrishna.

“Yes.”

Ṛṣi Dhaumya entrusted Upamanyu with the care of the āśrama’s cows.

Each morning,

he led the cows into the forest.

Each evening,

he brought them safely back to the āśrama.

One evening,

when Upamanyu returned with the cows,

he bowed before his guru.

Dhaumya looked at him.

“My child…
day after day,
I see you growing stronger.
Tell me…
how do you sustain yourself?”

Upamanyu bowed.

“Bhagavan…
I live on bhikṣā.”

Dhaumya said,

“My child…
whatever bhikṣā you receive,
offer it to me first.
Only then may it be used.”

“So be it, Bhagavan.”

From that day,

whatever bhikṣā Upamanyu received,

he placed before his guru.

Dhaumya accepted all of it.

The days passed.

Each morning,

Upamanyu led the cows into the forest.

Each evening,

he returned with the herd and bowed before his guru.

One evening,

Dhaumya looked at him.

“My child…
I take all the bhikṣā you receive, leaving nothing.
Yet,
day after day,
I see you growing stronger.
Tell me…
how do you sustain yourself?”

Upamanyu replied,

“Bhagavan…
after offering my bhikṣā to you,
I go once more and seek bhikṣā again.”

Dhaumya said,

“My child…
that is not the way.
Others too live by bhikṣā.
Do not diminish their share.”

“So be it, Bhagavan.”

From that day,

Upamanyu never sought a second round of bhikṣā.

The days passed.

Each morning,

Upamanyu led the cows into the forest.

Each evening,

he returned with the herd and bowed before his guru.

One evening,

Dhaumya looked at him.

“My child…
you no longer seek a second round of bhikṣā.
Yet,
day after day,
I see you growing stronger.
Tell me…
how do you sustain yourself?”

Upamanyu replied,

“Bhagavan…
I live on the milk of these cows.”

Dhaumya said,

“My child…
I have given you no such permission.”

“So be it, Bhagavan.”

From that day,

Upamanyu drank no more milk.

The days passed.

Each morning,

Upamanyu led the cows into the forest.

Each evening,

he returned with the herd and bowed before his guru.

One evening,

Dhaumya looked at him.

“My child…
you no longer drink the cows’ milk.
Yet,
day after day,
I see you growing stronger.
Tell me…
how do you sustain yourself?”

Upamanyu replied,

“Bhagavan…
when the calves drink,
foam gathers at their mouths.
I live on that foam.”

Dhaumya said,

“My child…
Even that belongs to them.
Do not deprive them of it.”

“So be it, Bhagavan.”

From that day,

Upamanyu drank the foam no more.

From that day,

Upamanyu ate no bhikṣā.

He sought no second round of bhikṣā.

He drank no milk.

He drank no foam.

Yet,

each morning,

he led the cows into the forest.

Each evening,

he brought them safely back to the āśrama.

One day,

as he wandered through the forest,

hunger overcame him.

He saw the leaves of an arka plant.

Thinking they might sustain him,

he ate them.

The leaves were harsh and bitter.

Before long,

his eyes could no longer see.

Blind,

he wandered through the forest.

And as he searched for his way,

he fell into a deep well.

That evening,

the cows returned to the āśrama.

But Upamanyu

did not.

The sun was already

sinking low.

Dhaumya looked around.

“Upamanyu has not returned.”

He turned to the other students.

“Come.
Let us go and look for him.”

Together,

they entered the forest.

As they searched,

Dhaumya called out,

“Upamanyu…
where are you,my child?”

From somewhere in the distance,

a voice answered,

“Bhagavan…
I have fallen into a well.”

Dhaumya followed the sound of his voice.

“My child… how did this happen?”

Upamanyu replied,

“Bhagavan…
driven by hunger, I ate the leaves of an arka plant.
They took away my sight.
Blind,
I wandered through the forest and fell into this well.”

Dhaumya said,

“My child…
offer your praise to the Aśvinī Kumāras — the divine physicians.
They will restore your sight.”

“So be it, Bhagavan.”

From within the well,

Upamanyu began to praise the Aśvinī Kumāras.

Before long, the twin gods appeared before him.

They held out a sacred cake.

“My child,”

they said, “Your hymn has reached us. Take this. Eat it.”

Upamanyu folded his hands.

“Bhagavans…
I cannot eat this without first offering it to my guru.”

The Aśvinī Kumāras looked upon him with delight.

“My child…
your devotion to your guru
has pleased us.
Your sight shall return.
You shall attain the highest good.”

At once,

Upamanyu’s sight was restored.

He came to his guru and bowed before him.

Dhaumya’s heart warmed toward Upamanyu.

“My child…
just as the Aśvinī Kumāras have said,
so shall it be.
The Vedas and the Dharmaśāstras shall shine forth within you.”

The story came to an end.

For a while,

the children sat quietly.

Then Neelakrishna looked at Appaa.

“Appaa…
Upamanyu never questioned his guru.
Every time, he simply said,
‘So be it, Bhagavan.’
Why?”

Niveditaa thought for a moment.

“Maybe…
he trusted his guru completely.”

Appaa nodded.

“Yes.
That is called śraddhā.”

Niveditaa looked at Appaa.

“Appaa…
when Āruṇi returned,
Ṛṣi Dhaumya blessed him.
When Upamanyu returned,
he said the very same words.
‘The Vedas and the Dharmaśāstras shall shine forth within you.’
Why didn’t he say,
‘You will become a great scholar in the Vedas’?”

Appaa said,

“Some things we learn with the mind.
The Vedas are different.
They shine within,
when we become ready for them.”
