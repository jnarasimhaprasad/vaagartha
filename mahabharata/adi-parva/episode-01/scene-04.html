Vyāsa · Gaṇeśa

Vyāsa Meets Gaṇeśa

Vyāsa Meets Gaṇeśa

Appaa smiled.

“He did.”

For a few moments, no one spoke.

Then he continued.

“Sauti said—
Following Lord Brahmā's words, Bhagavān Vyāsa remembered Gaṇapati.
He did not send a messenger.
He did not go in search of him.
He simply remembered him.
And the very moment he was remembered...
Gaṇapati came.”

Neither of the children spoke.

Niveditaa's eyes had grown wide.

Appaa continued.

“Vyāsa rose to receive him.
He welcomed him with great reverence.
He worshipped him.
Then he said—
‘O Gaṇanāyaka...
I wish to bring the Mahābhārata into written form.
Will you become its scribe?’”

Neelakrishna smiled.

“He agreed... didn't he?”

Appaa nodded.

“He did.
But not immediately.”

Both children looked at him.

“Gaṇapati said—
‘I shall write.
But only on one condition.
Your narration must never stop.
The moment you pause...
I shall stop writing.’”

Neelakrishna blinked.

“So Vyāsa had to keep speaking continuously?”

“Yes.”

Appaa smiled.

“But Vyāsa too placed one condition.”

Niveditaa leaned forward.

“What was it?”

“He said—
‘You must never write a single verse unless you have understood it completely.’”

Silence returned to the room.

After a while Neelakrishna said,

“That is fair.”

Appaa nodded.

“Vyāsa agreed.
Gaṇapati accepted his condition.
And thus the writing of the Mahābhārata began.”

For a few moments the room was quiet.

Then Appaa continued.

“And so the writing of the Mahābhārata began.
Vyāsa dictated.
Gaṇapati wrote.
Verse after verse flowed without interruption.”

He paused.

“But once in a while, Vyāsa would compose a very special kind of verse.”

Neelakrishna looked up.

“What kind of verse?”

“A kūṭa-śloka.”

The children waited.

Appaa continued.

“A kūṭa-śloka is a verse that hides its meaning.
The words seem to point in one direction...
while the real meaning quietly waits in another.”

Neelakrishna smiled.

“So it is like a riddle?”

“A little,” Appaa said. “But richer than a riddle.
It rewards patience.
You have to linger with it before it opens.”

He smiled.

“And that is exactly what Vyāsa wanted.
While Gaṇapati paused to relish such a verse before writing it down...
Vyāsa quietly composed the verses that were to follow.”

Appaa looked at both of them.

“Shall we try one?”

Both children nodded eagerly.

Appaa slowly recited—

“Nadī...
Jalam...
Keśava...
Nārī...
Ketu...”

Neelakrishna immediately sat up.

“Nadī...
River.”

Niveditaa smiled.

“And jalam...
Well... rivers do have water!”

Neelakrishna nodded.

“So far, so good.”

He continued.

“Keśava...
Kṛṣṇa.”

Niveditaa's eyes lit up.

“Maybe...
because Kṛṣṇa loved the Yamunā!”

Neelakrishna smiled.

“That fits!”

He counted on his fingers.

“River...
Water...
Kṛṣṇa...”

Niveditaa leaned forward.

“And nārī...
The gopīs!”

She laughed.

“They're always around Kṛṣṇa!”

Neelakrishna grinned.

“I think we've almost solved it!”

He looked at the last word.

“Ketu...”

His smile slowly faded.

“Ketu...?”

Niveditaa thought for a moment.

“The planet?”

Neelakrishna shook his head.

“No...”

“Then...
a flag?”

He frowned.

“That doesn't fit either.”

Both of them looked at Appaa.

He let the silence linger.

Then a gentle smile spread across his face.

“Exactly.
That is what a kūṭa-śloka feels like.
Now listen differently.
Not—
Nadī...
Jalam...
Keśava...
Nārī...
Ketu...
but—
Nadīja...
Laṅkeśavanāri...
Ketuḥ.”

The children listened without speaking.

“Nadīja—
the son of the river Gaṅgā.
Bhīṣma.
Laṅkeśavanāri—
the enemy who laid waste to the lord of Laṅkā's Aśokavana.
Hanumān.
Ketuḥ—
the banner.
Hanumān upon the banner.
Kapidhvaja.
Arjuna.”

Neelakrishna's face suddenly brightened.

“Oh!”

Appaa smiled.

“Droṇa was speaking to Bhīṣma.
Without anyone else understanding.
He was saying—
‘O Son of Gaṅgā...
Look...
Arjuna has arrived.’”

The room became quiet again.

Appaa smiled.

“Gaṇapati paused only for a moment.
That little pause was enough.
Vyāsa quietly shaped the verses that followed.”
